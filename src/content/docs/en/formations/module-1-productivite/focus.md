Very few people realize how much focus shapes their lives. It is the difference between the student who prepares for an exam in 2 hours and the one who spends 8 - for the same result.

> Focus is not an inborn talent. Like a muscle, it gets stronger with regular training.

## Why focus is critical

- **24 minutes**: the average time it takes to recover focus after an interruption
- **35,000 decisions/day**: that is how many your brain makes - every distraction wastes some of that capacity
- Multitasking does not work - your brain switches between tasks, it does not do them in parallel
- Information overload scatters attention - you have to filter signal from noise

## Deep Work

Cal Newport's concept: the ability to concentrate without distraction on a cognitively demanding task. It is a superpower in our distraction economy.

### How to get into Deep Work

1. **The night before**: write the 1-2 tasks you will do tomorrow in a journal
2. **In the morning**: wake up before everyone else, do not touch your phone
3. **Prepare the space**: make your bed, clear your desk
4. **Block the time**: turn off notifications, close Slack, keep your phone out of reach
5. **Work on ONE task** with short breaks
6. **If you get stuck**: change environment, meditate for 5 minutes, or use the 2-minute rule (do it for at least 2 minutes)
7. **In the afternoon**: go for a walk in nature to recharge

## Eliminating distractions

### Digital distractions

| Problem | Solution |
|---------|----------|
| Phone notifications | Remove them all, create dedicated slots (30 min morning/evening) |
| Social media | Blockers: StayFocusd, News Feed Eradicator |
| YouTube clickbait | DeArrow (crowd-sourced extension) |
| Constant email | Inbox Zero + scheduled email windows (CloudHQ Pause Gmail) |
| Blue light at night | AutoDarkMode, Dark Reader |

### Physical distractions

- **Keep your phone in another room** during deep work
- **Keep your workspace tidy** - a messy desk = a messy mind
- **Use dedicated places**: do not work in bed (your brain should associate bed = sleep)

## The couch test

When you collapse on the couch, what do you reach for: a pillow because you need sleep, or your phone because you want to scroll?

- **Pillow** -> you need rest. Sleep is the priority.
- **Phone** -> you are chasing dopamine. Choose a higher-quality source of dopamine (exercise, creation, learning).

## Managing mental energy

Your mental energy is a reservoir that drains as the day goes on. Every task consumes some of it - emails use a little, a presentation uses a lot.

### Manage your energy budget

- **Schedule** high-energy tasks when your tank is full
- **Batch** similar tasks together
- **Insert** energizing practices: 5 min of breathing, stretching, a glass of water
- **End** each day with some energy left - not completely drained
- **Experiment**: do morning meetings leave you exhausted? Does an afternoon walk recharge you?

## Beating procrastination

Procrastination is not your enemy - it is a battle between your limbic system and your prefrontal cortex, both trying to protect you from discomfort.

### Why you procrastinate on what matters most

The more you want something, the more pressure you place on yourself. That pressure creates stress, which makes you postpone the simple actions that would bring you closer to your goal.

### The solution: focus on the process, not the result

1. **Identify** the tasks you keep putting off
2. **Eat the frog**: tackle the hardest task first thing in the morning
3. **Alternate**: dreaded task, then pleasant task
4. **Segment**: break it into 5-10 minute micro-actions
5. **2-minute rule**: if you do not want to do it, commit to just 2 minutes

### "Positive procrastination"

When you procrastinate, ask yourself: *why do I not want to do this?* Listen to your subconscious answer. Sometimes it is useful information.

## Inbox Zero

Your inbox should not be your to-do list. Kelly Stocker's Gmail system (based on David Allen's GTD):

1. Enable **Multiple Inboxes** in Gmail
2. Create 5 categories with colored stars: Follow Up, Awaiting Reply, Delegated, Reference, To Read
3. Each morning: **star** important emails, **archive everything else**
4. Result: inbox zero in seconds, every day

**Additional tools:**
- **Superhuman** - ultra-fast email
- **Shortwave** - intelligent email
- **SlickInbox** - all your newsletters in a dedicated app

## Attention, according to neuroscience

### Inattentional blindness

The gorilla experiment (Simons & Chabris): when we focus on a task, we can literally fail to notice a gorilla walking across the screen. Our impression that we perceive a rich, detailed scene is an illusion.

### The central bottleneck

The prefrontal cortex ensures that tasks requiring focus are handled **one at a time**. Having two active intentions at once creates a neural conflict - that is the scientific reason multitasking does not work.

### ADHD & hyperfocus

The ability to hyperfocus is a superpower often associated with ADHD. Intense concentration, remarkable results - but the challenge is controlling when and on what hyperfocus switches on.

Hyperfocus is not a flaw to fix; it is an asset to channel. The trap is that it often turns on for whatever is most stimulating (games, social media) instead of what matters. The key is to create an environment where important tasks become the most stimulating option available.

### Distraction as escape

A Timothy Wilson study (University of Virginia) found that **67% of men would rather give themselves electric shocks than sit alone with their thoughts**. Distraction is not a willpower problem - it is an escape from mental discomfort.

**3 steps to resist distraction:**
1. **Notice the urge** - observe the moment you reach for your phone or open a new tab
2. **See thoughts as thoughts** - not as orders. "I want to check Instagram" is not "I must check Instagram"
3. **Bring attention back to the present** - breathe, and refocus on the task in front of you

### Managing mental load

- **List everything** you need to do so it leaves your head
- **Rank by priority** and create a sequence
- **Short goals**: 20-minute blocks per task
- **Adapt your breaks** to the intensity of the task
- **Mandatory recovery time** (reading, sport, walking)
