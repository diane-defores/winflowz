## Redefining success

The classic definition of success - six figures, a big house, a nice car - no longer fits. We are coming out of a pandemic, a climate crisis, and technological revolutions. Our values have changed, and our definition of success should change with them.

**What success can look like today:**

- **Work/life harmony** - not "balance" (a myth), but a career that complements your life instead of devouring it
- **Personal growth** - measure success by what you have learned and overcome
- **Health and wellbeing** - in a hustle culture, making health a priority is a radical act
- **Fulfillment** - not constant happiness (unrealistic), but a life that feels meaningful

### How to redefine your success

1. **Identify your core values** - what actually makes you happy?
2. **Set holistic goals** - not just financial ones: relationships, health, learning
3. **Practice gratitude** - sometimes success is seeing what you already have
4. **Reassess regularly** - your definition will evolve, and that is normal

## The Problem x Purpose duo

Productivity without direction is just activity. To move forward with clarity, you need two things.

### Find your problem

A clear problem removes hesitation. When you know exactly what you need to solve, the next actions become obvious.

> Without a problem to solve, you drift: one more book, one more podcast, and nothing changes.

**Exercise**: complete the sentence *"I am tired of..."* - that is often where your real problem is hiding. What frustrates you? What could be improved in your life or your industry?

### Define your purpose

The problem gives you focus. The purpose gives you motion. It is your compass - the reason you get out of bed in the morning.

Your purpose can be:
- Helping others
- Achieving financial freedom
- Creating something beautiful
- Giving your family a better life

The important thing is that it resonates with your core values.

### The synergy between both

When a clear problem meets a powerful purpose, motivation stops being an occasional visitor and becomes a permanent resident.

Procrastination stops. Fake progress stops. Lukewarm motivation stops.

**In practice:**
1. **Identify the problem** blocking your progress
2. **Connect it to your deeper purpose**
3. **Let both guide** your daily actions
4. **Stay adaptable** - life is unpredictable
5. **Celebrate every step forward**, even the small ones

## The problem as a compass - two stories

### Airbnb: an air mattress and unpaid rent

Brian Chesky and Joe Gebbia could not afford their rent in San Francisco. Their problem was visceral, concrete, immediate. They put an air mattress in their living room and charged travelers to stay there. A global business was born from a rent problem.

### Tom's Shoes: a trip that changed everything

Blake Mycoskie saw children without shoes in Argentina. His problem was clear - and so was his purpose. He created the *one for one* model: one pair bought, one pair donated.

**The productivity lesson**: in both cases, problem clarity removed hesitation. No endless brainstorming, no analysis paralysis. Obvious problem -> obvious action.

> Imagine you are chained to railway tracks and a train is coming. You do not read a book about 19th-century trains - you look for the key. Clear problem = clear action.

## Passion and profit: the collector's trap

Two video game collectors at a flea market. The first hoards for himself. The second shares his finds, documents the journey, builds a community, and monetizes it.

**The lesson**: a passion that is not monetized is not a project - it is a hobby. That is not a bad thing, but do not confuse the two. If you want to live from your passion:

- **Document the journey** instead of trying to play the expert
- **Share in real time** - authenticity beats self-appointed expertise
- **Monetize early** (affiliate links, content, services) - even modestly, it validates the direction

## Meaning vs performance: the impossible ratio?

Content creator Victor Ferry points to a tension every entrepreneur feels: **should you pursue meaning or performance?** Trying to do both at once is often contradictory.

His solution: an intentional ratio - roughly 3/4 performance, 1/4 meaning. Some content is meant to perform (sell, convert, grow). Other content is meant to feed the soul (explore, share, inspire). Trying to mix both in every piece usually fails at both.

**In practice**: accept that not everything can be deep *and* viral. Alternate modes.

## The create-sell tension (the Jean Rivere case)

French infopreneur Jean Rivere lived this tension for years. His best financial results came from aggressive selling - but he lost his creative spark doing it. Covid pushed him to buy a farm on impulse and create authentic content - but his catalog became incoherent. Cycle after cycle: sell or create, never both in harmony.

**The productivity lesson**: if your process creates inner dissonance, no productivity system will save you. Alignment between what you do and who you are is not a luxury - it is a prerequisite. Many creators feel this tension without naming it. Naming it is the first step toward solving it.

## Fall in love with the process

The more you enjoy running your business, the more likely you are to succeed in the long run. You get discouraged less, procrastinate less, and think more clearly.

**The problem**: even in a dream job, many tasks remain tedious. Admin, accounting, sales - those chores can drain the fire.

### 5 levers to keep the fire alive

**1. Simplify (the 80/20 rule)**
20% of your effort produces 80% of your results. Identify that 20% and cut the rest.

**2. Standardize**
Create procedures for repetitive tasks. A tested checklist beats Monday-morning improvisation.

**3. Batch**
Do not do bookkeeping a little bit every day - block two days at the beginning of the month, do it all at once, and free your mind for everything else. Same for admin: one list, cleared every Monday.

**4. Delegate**
Other people are often better suited to the tasks you dislike. You gain quality and time for the work that actually moves the needle.

**5. Automate**
Automation tools are everywhere. Investing in automating repetitive tasks is one of the best returns on investment you can make.

## Set your boundaries

You can only give others what you already have. Taking care of yourself is not selfish - it is the condition for being useful. Learn to say no to protect your momentum and your focus.

The upside of being independent: the freedom to step off the beaten path, explore, practice, and learn. But that freedom is worthless if you spend it trying to please everyone.

**In practice:**
- Identify requests that drain your energy without serving your purpose
- Set clear boundaries around your availability (hours, communication channels)
- Protect your deep work blocks like non-negotiable appointments
- Remember: every "yes" to someone else is a "no" to yourself
